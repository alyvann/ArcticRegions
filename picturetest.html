Tracing The Arctic Regions

A Project by Stanford's Center for Spatial and Textual Analysis

Text and Concept by George Philip LeBourdais, Web Design by Frank Zheng, Animations by Olivia Booker

Tracing The Arctic Regions is a digital art history project developed at the Center for Spatial and Textual Analysis at the Department of Art & Art History at Stanford University. It charts the geography of The Arctic Regions, a rare 1873 book of photographs of the west coast of Greenland created by the American marine painter William Bradford (1823-1892).

Today, The Arctic Regions is celebrated as one of the last great examples of nineteenth-century photographic books, in which each collodion on glass negative was printed by hand and then individually pasted or “tipped in” to the pages. This labor intensive process would wither in the 1880s in favor of the cheaper and more efficient methods of mechanical printing that were being developed.

Curiously, it was Bradford’s talents as a painter that led to his contribution to photographic history. By the 1860s he was considered America’s preeminent painter of the Arctic, overshadowed only by Frederic Edwin Church. This was a remarkable achievement, given the nation’s pervasive cultural and political fascination with “the Frozen North” at that time. The map of “Northern Regions” shown below, part of an 1855 atlas by the renowned New York mapmaker Joseph Hutchins Colton, represents the extent of most people’s knowledge of Arctic space at the time. There was much terra incognita, to say nothing of the ice.

When the 1860s began, the two most popular books in American homes were the Bible and Arctic Explorations, a memoir of the famous explorer Dr. Elisha Kent Kane’s search for the lost expedition of Sir John Franklin. Arctic interest flared further in the mid-nineteenth century through the rise of new forms of mass media: photography, lectures with lantern slide projections, and the immersive format of “panorama,” wide-angle visualizations that created the sense of being surrounded by a remote place.

Public interest in the Arctic reflected the imperial aspirations of the U.S. government. The United States laid no land claims in the Arctic until 1867, when it acquired a million and a half acres of land from Russia in the Alaska Purchase; by 1880, nearly half of the Arctic was considered in American hands.

Seven trips to Arctic waters for artistic research and study made Bradford more than just an intrepid, itinerant artist; he became a recognized authority on Arctic geography. A member of American Geographical Society, he was invited to give lectures on his knowledge of the Frozen North and was an advisor to the official U.S. Arctic Expedition of 1879-81 on the ill-fated U.S.S Jeannette.

Bradford made the last and most ambitious of these seven trips to the Arctic in 1869. With financial backing from the New York financier Le Grand Lockwood, a patron of the well-known painter of the West, Albert Bierstadt, Bradford had secured a 350-ton steam ship, an experienced Newfoundland crew, and most significantly two Boston-based photographers, John Dunmore and George Critcherson.

Dunmore and Critcherson produced scores of images, under Bradford’s artistic supervision, of Greenland’s western coast from July until September 1869. The 1873 printing of The Arctic Regions in London combined 141 of these images with Bradford’s narrative text, creating a reading experience that flowed between word and image.

Yet, The Arctic Regions itself contains no map, a form of spatial knowledge that would seem standard for this kind exploration. The decision to exclude a cartographic view of the Arctic points back to the first word in the title of the project: tracing. While “trace” implies genealogy, continuum, and a line leading from one thing to another, it also signifies, from the Latin root tractus, an area of indefinite extent. Tracing something, in other words, means accepting that much of it may remain unknown.

This digital art history project identifies the locations of the most striking and important of these images, and at the same time allows others to remain shrouded in ambiguity. While the ambiguous locations of floating icebergs and grinding glaciers make many of the photographs impossible to locate, many images of specific places and people - including many portraits of the Kalaallit, the native Greenland Inuit - are vivid representations of the Arctic. Following the course of the Panther through Bradford’s text and Dunmore and Critcherson’s photographs, we can “trace” the path of The Arctic Regions and learn why the arctic and its ice were so important to American culture in the nineteenth-century.

Cape Desolation

The narrative of The Arctic Regions unfolds chronologically in the summer of 1869. After an introductory chapter on ancient Greenland borrowed from Benjamin Franklin de Costa’s 1868 book, Pre-Colombian Discovery of America by the Northmen, Bradford begins his description on July 3, 1869, when the steamship Panther left the harbor of St. John’s, Newfoundland. The United States flag was flying on the fore topgallant mast; though undertaken “solely for the purposes of art” as Bradford noted in the introduction, this expedition was also self-consciously nationalistic.

On July 9, the fog broke and the crew sighted its first view of Greenland: an imposing chunk of rock that had been since 1585 known as Cape Desolation. Circling around the headland were evocatively shaped icebergs, the natural forms that had attracted Bradford to the Arctic in the first place.

On previous expeditions to Labrador, Bradford had been enraptured by the close study of icebergs around working ships. Noting their angles and contours quickly in sketchbooks, he applied these studies and experience to the paintings of the icebergs for which he had become so well known. The painting Labrador Fishing Boats near Cape Charles shows Bradford’s skill in this mode. While he began his career painting ship’s portraits, boats in this view are pushed to the side, while a pointed iceberg takes center stage. Human labor coexists peacefully with natural forces here, but the former warily gives the latter a wide berth.

Bradford’s description of the first encounter with Greenland’s coast conjures a more mysterious geography. Sweeping fog pulls the coastline and out of visibility, while icebergs emerge suddenly and dangerously close to the ship.

While excitement in polar expedition had reached a fever pitch after Kane’s publication of Arctic Explorations in 1855, public perception had shifted a great deal by 1869. Interest in the Arctic evaporated after the outbreak of the Civil War. Many trips north were still in search of Sir John Franklin’s expedition, lost since 1845. As Americans began to hear of men dying for important causes at the battles of Harper’s Ferry and Bull Run, stories of self-elected hardship in the Arctic seemed foolish and wasteful.

With stories of the war and the difficulties of Reconstruction still at hand in 1869, Bradford’s text was probably shrewd not to associate the arctic with the language of battle or suffering. In fact, this first encounter with icebergs around Cape Desolation establishes them as surprisingly ambivalent figures. When a stiff wind makes it too dangerous to anchor around the coast, the crew decides to make fast to an iceberg for safe harbor. Unlike the calamitous collision between the Titanic and an iceberg in 1912, in 1869 the Panther dovetailed with ice. Their trajectories were united, however briefly.
