Seventy-five Degrees North: Finding the Edge

The polar bear hunt stands as a point of high drama in The Arctic Regions. It also served an essential role in the expedition’s narrative. By depicting a struggle for life and death, the episode set the stage for the apex of the expedition and the mortal threats of the uppermost Arctic. While the ship Panther had crossed the Arctic Circle at 66° N latitude weeks before, Bradford and his crew wanted to press on. In an era fixated on superlatives and firsts, reaching the farthest point possible would have been a thrill for members of the expedition and an accomplishment they could parlay into future opportunities. But according to the self-realization parables at the heart of Arctic narratives, reaching the highest point wasn’t just a means to an end; it had become an end in itself.

By reaching Melville Bay in 1869, the crew of the Panther notched an impressive if qualified achievement. Entering the pack ice at 75° N placed them, by Bradford’s estimation, “much further, certainly, than anyone had ever before ventured for the purposes of art, and quite as far as it was safe to go unless prepared to winter in the ice, which we were not.”[1]

The statement acknowledges explorers who had previously reached higher latitudes. In the 1850s, Elisha Kent Kane became one of America’s first national celebrities thanks to the books that chronicled his experiences on the Grinnell Expeditions to find Sir John Franklin, journeys that brought him to 80ºN in the Kennedy Channel. In 1871, before the photographs from Bradford’s 1869 expedition were printed for The Arctic Regions, the American explorer Charles Francis Hall died of mysterious circumstances before his crew was trapped in the ice of Smith Sound, the channel separating Greenland from Canada around 78º N. A New York Times story on the tragedy from July 20, 1873 even cast doubt on the merit of such endeavors: “Whether it is worth while to risk the lives of brave men in following what may be but a fleeting mirage, is a question which the death of Capt. Hall and the sufferings of his gallant companions will incline most people to answer in the negative.”

These expeditions and the narratives that glorified them demonstrate a shifting rationale for Arctic exploration. In many ways, Bradford’s photographic journey ran parallel to the far-reaching surveys of the American West organized by the U.S. government in the 1860s and 70s. Balancing broad, geographic views with illustrative points of interest, photographers like Timothy O’Sullivan developed a distinctive pictorial economy with a limited number of prints. Yet Bradford was not exploring U.S. lands, nor was he constrained by the bureaucratic demands levied on his counterparts. The Arctic held a nebulous strategic value, but its mystique on stage of colonial exploration was inestimable.

It was not necessary to discover something of tangible economic value, a shipping route through the Northwest Passage, or rare minerals like cryolite that could be mined for profit; just returning from the Arctic was a heroic accomplishment on its own terms. For instance, Dr. Isaac Israel Hayes had been invited to join Bradford’s expedition thanks to his other Arctic experiences, including Kane’s Grinnell expedition.[2]

Hayes was also interested in verifying the presence of an Open Polar Sea--a misguided hypothesis arguing for an ice-free, tropical body of water concealed by icebergs and glaciers around the North Pole, like an oasis in the desert. While this theory certainly involved finding the edge of Arctic ice, as its credibility eroded stories of human resilience and national pride remained enough to justify the trip. In this sense, the Arctic became a place for self-realization. You went there to find your limits. While many succeeded in finding this edge, only the fortunate returned to describe it.

In paying deference to higher latitude heroes, however, Bradford was also staking his claim as America’s preeminent artist. Going “further than anyone ever had for the purposes of art” invoked the travels of Frederic Edwin Church, the preeminent landscape painter in the United States at the time. In 1861, Church had caused a stir with the exhibition of his painting The Icebergs, a canvas based on his own expedition to Labrador in 1859. This trip, floridly chronicled by Louis Legrand Noble in his book After Icebergs with a Painter, further cemented Church as one of America’s great artist-explorers.

While Bradford might have accepted that his own paintings of first-hand Arctic experience would remain in the shadow of Church’s extraordinary talent, his writing in The Arctic Regions positions this expedition as a more dangerous and thus more impressive undertaking. It must have been all the more gratifying, then, when Church later purchased negatives from The Arctic Regions for later study.

These twinned impulses of artist and explorer combine in the dramatic photographs taken during this leg of Bradford’s journey. As the Panther neared 75ºN around August 12, a cold snap caused the broken chunks of sea ice to freeze together. Summer did not last long at those latitudes, and the open leads in the ice that had allowed the ship to explore a spectacular, castle-like berg quickly closed up. Some ships that had been nipped, or stuck, in the ice in similar situations had been trapped through the winter, a scenario for which the expedition was unprepared.

Bradford and his photographers chose to document this anxious episode with a suite of photographs that show the Panther imperiled in the ice. Strangely, because progress through such situations can be slow, there was ample time to make pictures. Chapter XIII--in which the ship reaches its northernmost point, gets trapped in the ice for three days, and then finally under full steam, breaks free--contains 21 photographs, more images than any other episode. While many of these photographs are beautiful, many of them also resemble each other. Moreover, because most depict the ship at a distance, they lack the sense of action or urgency that the text is at pains to convey.

The effect is a profound tension between text and image that is a fitting climax for a narrative about continuity. The photographs, which are themselves like frozen instants in time, portray a ship (and potentially its crew) frozen as well. Could there be a more apt use for the phrase “frozen still”? By depicting the edge of survival for Arctic travelers, the photographs also draw a line where movement stops and stillness reigns.
